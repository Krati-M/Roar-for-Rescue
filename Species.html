Bengal Tiger

Panthera Tigris Tigris



Common Name: Bengal Tiger

Scientific Name: Panthera Tigris Tigris

The King of the Jungle

The Bengal Tiger, panthera Tigris Tigris, is the largest of all Asian big cats. It can grow to be 6-10 ft in length, 220-660 pounds in weight, and live up to 20 years with the Raj having the longest lifespace of 23 years in the wild. Tigers rely primarily on sight and sound for hunting, and typically hunt alone, being solitary animals, by stalking the prey. They are the apex predators in their ecosystems, and play a significant role in maintaining biodiversity. On average 2 to 4 cubs are born in each litter, with cubs gaining their independence by 2 years of age, and sexual maturity by 3-5 years of age.

Fun fact: Tigers can consume more than 80 pounds of meat in one meal.

Threat: Habitat Loss

The Bengal tigers have lost over 95% of their historical habitat range through urbanization, construction, deforestation, degradation and fragmentation in the last century. Bengal tigers are very territorial and require large swaths of areas to hunt, live and stay healthy. Fragmentation and clearing of lands for logging and agriculture forced tigers to live on small, scattered islands of habitat closer to human population and urban areas that can support fewer tigers. All these factors make tigers more vulnerable to poaching, inbreeding, pose a barrier to their movement and hunting, and more susceptible to human threats such as disease. Habitat fragmentation also causes the population of their prey to decline

Threat: Hunting and Poaching

Hunting and poaching of the Bengal Tiger and its prey such as Deers is arguably the biggest reason for decline in their population. Poaching for illegal trade in high-value Tiger products including skins, bones, meat, and tonics, and it is the main reason for their disappearance from suitable habitats. In 2008, the main loss of tigers was due to hunting and in southeast Asia. Another big reason that tigers are periodically being hunted more is that, as prey population declines, Tigers are more likely to travel to urban and human settlements to find food, which can include humans, livestock, pets, causing frustration from the people who, in turn, kill and capture them, leading to their death. Some may even end up on “sell” in black markets. Current estimates from WWF indicate that there are more than 8,000 tigers being held in more than 200 centers in East and Southeast Asia, with roughly three-quarters of these tigers located in China, specifically. These serve as covers and centers for illegal poaching, transport and breeding

Threat: Climate Change

The most populated region of Bengal tigers in the Sundarbans, a mangrove forest and swamp shared by India and Bangladesh. This is the only coastal region that Bengal Tigers currently reside and due to rising sea levels, storm surges, wildfires, heat waves causing temperature fluctuation and wing damage, all due to climate change, can completely destroy the remaining of the Bengal Tiger’s habitat, by 2050, according to recent patterns which is only getting worse. These changing conditions cause tigers to migrate to places closer to human settlements leading to more human-wildlife conflicts.
